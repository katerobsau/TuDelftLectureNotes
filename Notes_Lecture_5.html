Turning Point: Expectation

First Question

Question: Is \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) is the most likely value of \(X\)?

Answer: No.

Why? The expection does not need to be a value in the support of \(X\). The expection is just the centre of mass. Consider the counterexample where \(X\) is a random variable representing the the toss of a 6 sided die.

\[\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i = 1}^6 x \mathbb{P}(X = x) = \sum_{i = 1}^6 x \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \tfrac{1}{6} + \tfrac{2}{6} + \dots + \tfrac{6}{6} = 3.5\] A roll of 3.5 isn't even possible! In this example, all values are also equally likely.

First Question (ctd.)

Question: Is the \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) is always positive?

Answer: No.

Why? Again consider a counterexmple. For example a normal distribution, \(N(-10,1)\), the centre of mass of the distribution and the expection is -10.

Second Question

Let \(X\) be a discrete RV with pmf

\[ p(-1) = \tfrac{1}{5}, \quad p(0) = \tfrac{2}{5} \quad \text{and} \quad p(1) = \tfrac{2}{5},\] and let \(Y = X^2\).

Question: What is the expection of \(X\) and \(Y\)?

Answer \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) and \(\tfrac{3}{5}\).

Why?

\[\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i \in \{-1,0,1\}} x \mathbb{P}(X = x) = -1 \cdot \tfrac{1}{5} + 0 \cdot \tfrac{2}{5} + 1 \cdot \tfrac{2}{5} = \tfrac{1}{5}\]

Second question (ctd.)

The pmf for \(Y\) is then:

\[ p(0) = \tfrac{2}{5} \quad \text{and} \quad p(1) = \tfrac{3}{5}.\]

\[\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_{i \in \{0,1\}} y \mathbb{P}(Y = y) = 0 \cdot \tfrac{2}{5} + 1 \cdot \tfrac{3}{5} = \tfrac{3}{5}\]

Signal arriving

Expectation is the center of mass: so for \(U(0, 2\pi)\) it will be \(\pi\).

Show this mathematically: \[ \mathbb{E}[\theta] = \int_0^{2\pi} \theta f(\theta) \,\text{d}\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} \theta \cdot \tfrac{1}{2\pi} \,\text{d}\theta = \tfrac{\theta^2}{2} \tfrac{1}{2\pi} \Big|_0^{2\pi} = \frac{4\pi^2}{4\pi} = \pi \]
