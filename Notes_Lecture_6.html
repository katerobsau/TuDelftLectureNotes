Turning Point 1

Let \(X\) be a random variable with \(\mathbb{E}(X)=3\).

Question: What is \(\mathbb{E}(3X - 6)\)?

Answer: 3

Solution:

\[\mathbb{E}(3X - 6) = \mathbb{E}(3X) - 6 = 3\mathbb{E}(X) - 6 = 3\cdot3 - 6 = 3\]

Turning Point 2

Question: Is the function \(g(x) = 3x - 6\) convex and or concave?

Answer: Both!

Solution:

By Jensen's Inequality the function is concave if \[ \mathbb{E}[g(X)] \leq g(\mathbb{E}[X])\] and convex if \[ \mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X]).\]

Given we have equality \(\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = g(\mathbb{E}[X])\) the function is both! Can also show this by considering first and second derivatives.

Transforming Normal RVs

Motivational Example

  • Two scientists
  • Repeatedly taking temperature measurements as part of an experiment
  • Catch: Scientists are in different labs and using different measuring devices
  • Each of these instruments has some measurement error
  • Assume is normally distributed
  • One of these devices has hot bias of \(1^\circ C\), and a variance of \(0.5 ^\circ C\)
  • The other has cold bias of \(-1^\circ C\), and a variance of \(1.25 ^\circ C\)

Picture

To combine the measurements of both scientists, we need to account for the measurement error and transforming measurments to a common distribution (red dashed).

Another example

Consider an IQ test where participants have a mean score of 100 and variance of 15. If we assume the scores are approximately normally distributed then \(X \sim N(100, 15)\)

To standardise the test scores, we us the transform: \[ Z = \dfrac{X - \mu}{\sigma} = \dfrac{X - 100}{\sqrt{15}}.\] IQ scores are not strictly measurable so standardisation is needed across tests.

Turning Point 3a

Question: The joint distribution of RVs \(X\) and \(Y\) is always the product of the marginals

Answer: No

Why: If this statement was true then

\[\mathbb{P}(X \leq a, Y \leq b) = \mathbb{P}(X \leq a)\mathbb{P}(Y \leq b).\] and in the continuous case then we could separate the integrals

\[F_{XY}(a,b) = \int_{-\infty}^b\int_{-\infty}^a f(x,y) \text{d}x \text{d}y = \int_{-\infty}^a f(x) \text{d}x \int_{-\infty}^b f(y) \text{d}y = F_X(a)F_Y(b).\] However, this is clearly not possible for all functions \(f(x,y)\). Eg. If \(X \sim U(0, 1)\) and \(Y \sim U(0,x)\).

Turning Point 4a

Question: Is \(f_x(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y) \text{d}x\)?

Answer: No, the statements is false.

Why: To obtain the marginal density of \(x\) from the joint density we integrate over \(y\) not \(x\). It therefore should be \[f_x(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y) \text{d}y.\]

Turning Point 4b

Question: Is \(f_X(x) = \dfrac{\partial F_{XY}(x,y)}{\partial x}?\)

Answer: No, the statement is false.

Why: For distributions of a single random variable this is true \[f(x) = \dfrac{\text{d}F(x)}{\text{d}x},\] however for joint distributions such as \(F(x,y)\), \(y\) must also accounted for.
